Heroic Moments

The saves that became aviation legend — pilots who kept a crippled aircraft flying long enough to bring everyone home.

NTSB Verified
Jan 15, 2009US Airways Flight 1549Hudson River, NY

The Miracle on the Hudson

— January 2009

Ninety seconds after departing LaGuardia, Captain Chesley "Sully" Sullenberger's Airbus A320 struck a flock of Canada geese and lost both engines. With no runway within reach, he ditched the aircraft in the Hudson River. All 155 people aboard survived — a result the NTSB's final report credited to Sullenberger's decision-making, First Officer Jeffrey Skiles's checklist work, and a crew that had every passenger off the sinking aircraft in minutes.

NTSB Verified
Jul 19, 1989United Airlines Flight 232Sioux City, Iowa

The Captain Who Practiced For A Day Like This

— July 1989

When United 232's tail-mounted engine disintegrated and severed all three of the DC-10's independent hydraulic systems, Captain Al Haynes and his crew had no ailerons, no elevators and no rudder — only engine thrust to steer with. Using differential power between the two remaining wing engines, they brought the aircraft to a crash landing at Sioux Gateway Airport that 184 of the 296 people aboard survived, a result investigators later called a textbook case of crew resource management under conditions the aircraft was never designed to survive.

Jul 23, 1983Air Canada Flight 143Gimli, Manitoba

The Gimli Glider

— July 1983

A fuel-calculation error rooted in Air Canada's mid-conversion from imperial to metric units left Flight 143 with less than half the fuel its crew believed was aboard. Both engines flamed out at 41,000 feet. Captain Robert Pearson, a experienced glider pilot, sideslipped the powerless Boeing 767 onto a decommissioned airstrip at Gimli that was, unknown to him, being used that day as a drag-racing strip — and stopped just short of spectators.

Jun 24, 1982British Airways Flight 9Near Jakarta, Indonesia

"We Have A Small Problem"

— June 1982

Flying through what looked like ordinary night cloud, British Airways Flight 9 had in fact entered a cloud of volcanic ash from the eruption of Mount Galunggung — and lost all four engines. Captain Eric Moody's cabin announcement became aviation legend for its understatement: "We have a small problem. All four engines have stopped. We are doing our damnedest to get them going again." The crew glided the 747 from 37,000 feet, restarted the engines below the ash cloud, and landed safely in Jakarta.

Apr 17, 2018Southwest Airlines Flight 1380Philadelphia, PA

The Airline Captain Who Used To Fly Fighters

— April 2018

An uncontained engine failure ripped a hole in the fuselage of Southwest Flight 1380, partially depressurizing the cabin at 32,000 feet. Captain Tammie Jo Shults — one of the U.S. Navy's first female fighter pilots — flew the crippled 737 to an emergency landing in Philadelphia, staying calm enough on the radio that the controller asked if she needed any further assistance, to which she replied simply, "Could we have the medics meet us there on the runway as well?"

Nov 1, 2011LOT Polish Airlines Flight 16Warsaw, Poland

"Fly Like An Eagle, Land Like A Crow"

— November 2011

A hydraulic leak on LOT Flight 16 left the Boeing 767's landing gear stuck retracted. Captain Tadeusz Wrona circled Warsaw for over an hour to burn fuel while ground crews foamed the runway, then belly-landed the aircraft so smoothly that all 231 people aboard walked away without serious injury. The Polish press seized on his surname — Wrona, which means "crow" — coining the now-famous line that he'd landed like one.

Nov 4, 2010Qantas Flight 32Singapore

The A380 That Wouldn't Quit

— November 2010

Minutes after departing Singapore, one of Qantas Flight 32's four engines suffered an uncontained failure, spraying turbine debris through the wing and knocking out or degrading 21 of the aircraft's systems at once. Captain Richard de Crespigny's five-pilot flight deck crew worked through the failures methodically for nearly two hours before landing the A380 safely with all 469 people aboard unharmed.

Both Engines Dead, One Levee To Land On

— May 1988

TACA Flight 110 flew into a violent thunderstorm on approach to New Orleans and lost both engines to hail ingestion. With no power and no runway in reach, the crew glided the Boeing 737 onto a grass levee alongside the Mississippi River — then, remarkably, had the engines repaired well enough in place that the same aircraft was flown out again days later.

Jun 10, 1990British Airways Flight 5390Didcot, UK

The Captain Who Was Sucked Halfway Out The Window

— June 1990

An improperly bolted windscreen panel blew out at 17,300 feet, and the sudden decompression forced Captain Tim Lancaster headfirst out of the cockpit window, his knees the only thing still caught on the flight controls. Flight attendant Nigel Ogden grabbed his legs and held on until relieved by two colleagues, who kept the captain pinned in the window frame for twenty minutes until the first officer landed the aircraft. Lancaster survived and was flying again within five months.

ATSB Verified
Oct 7, 2008Qantas Flight 72Learmonth, Australia

The Computer That Tried To Crash The Plane

— October 2008

Cruising at 37,000 feet, a faulty air data sensor on Qantas Flight 72's A330 fed the flight computers erroneous angle-of-attack spikes, triggering two sudden, uncommanded pitch-down dives that flung unbelted passengers and crew into the cabin ceiling. The crew regained control and diverted to a remote airstrip at Learmonth. The Australian Transport Safety Bureau's final report — the only known case of its kind in 28 million flight hours — led Airbus to redesign the flight control software.

May 14, 2018Sichuan Airlines Flight 8633Sichuan Province, China

The Windshield That Blew Out At 30,000 Feet

— May 2018

The cockpit windshield of an Airbus A319 failed at 30,000 feet, sending the first officer partially out of the window before his seatbelt caught him. Wind noise was so severe the crew could barely hear each other, and most cockpit instruments were unreadable. Captain Liu Chuanjian, with over 11,000 flight hours, flew the aircraft down and landed it safely at Chengdu — a case now taught internationally as a benchmark for pilot composure under total sensory overload.

Life in the Cockpit

What the flight deck actually runs on — the rules, the routines and the quiet moments between the emergencies.

Skybrary Verified
Ongoing StandardEvery Commercial FlightWorldwide

What's Actually Happening During A "Normal" Landing

— Standard procedure

Every stabilized approach runs on a strict set of required cross-checks and verbal callouts between the pilot flying and the pilot monitoring — speed, sink rate, configuration and position, all confirmed aloud by a fixed altitude. If any one of them falls outside limits, the crew is trained to abandon the approach and go around, no discussion needed. Passengers rarely notice how much is being silently verified on every descent.

Ongoing StandardEvery Commercial FlightWorldwide

The Rejected Takeoff That Went Right

— Standard procedure

Below a specific speed calculated before every takeoff roll, crews are trained to reject and stop for almost any abnormality; above it, they're trained to keep going and sort the problem out in the air, because stopping becomes more dangerous than continuing. That single number — decided in seconds, rehearsed for years — is one of the most consequential judgment calls in aviation, and one passengers never see being made.

Aug 24, 2001Air Transat Flight 236Azores, Portugal

The Bush Pilot Who Glided An Airliner To Safety

— August 2001

Captain Robert Piché got his start landing light aircraft on short, rough strips in remote Quebec, and had logged serious hours as a glider pilot before ever flying for an airline. That background became the reason 306 people survived when a fuel leak left his Air Transat A330 completely powerless over the Atlantic — Piché flew a dead-stick descent and a series of S-turns to bleed off altitude, landing hard but safely in the Azores after gliding further than any airliner has before or since.

Jul 19, 1989United Airlines Flight 232Sioux City, Iowa

The Off-Duty Pilot In First Class

— July 1989

Dennis "Denny" Fitch was riding as a non-revenue passenger on United 232 — a DC-10 training check airman with nothing to do that day but fly home. When the aircraft lost all hydraulics, he went to the cockpit and spent the rest of the flight kneeling between the seats, working the throttle levers by hand to help steer with engine thrust alone. He later called the experience "the worst day of my life and the best day of my life," and spent much of his retirement flying sick patients to hospitals for free.

Effective 201414 CFR Part 117United States

The Regulation That Decides When A Pilot Can Fly

— Federal rule

Following a string of fatigue-linked accidents, U.S. regulators overhauled pilot flight-time and rest rules under 14 CFR Part 117, setting hard limits on duty periods and minimum rest based on how early a pilot reports and how many time zones are crossed. It's one of the least visible rules in commercial aviation, and one of the reasons a stranger in the left seat is required, by law, to not be exhausted.

Oct 14, 1947Bell X-1Mojave Desert, CA

Chuck Yeager And The Sound Barrier

— October 1947

Chuck Yeager flew the rocket-powered Bell X-1 past Mach 1 with two broken ribs he'd hidden from his superiors, using a sawed-off broom handle to lever the cockpit hatch shut because he couldn't reach it with his injured arm. It remains one of the defining test-pilot stories in aviation history — proof that the "sound barrier" was a limit of the aircraft, not the pilot.

1922–2016Test & Airshow PilotUnited States

"The Greatest Stick-And-Rudder Pilot Who Ever Lived"

— Career spanning decades

Bob Hoover was shot down over France in WWII, escaped a German POW camp by stealing an enemy fighter, then became Chuck Yeager's chase pilot for the X-1's supersonic flight before spending decades redefining aerobatic flying in a P-51 Mustang. Asked who the best pilot he'd ever seen was, Yeager didn't hesitate: "Bob Hoover. He's the greatest stick-and-rudder pilot who ever lived."

Jun 15, 1921First Licensed Black Woman PilotFrance

Bessie Coleman, "Queen Bess"

— June 1921

Barred from flight schools in the United States because she was Black and a woman, Bessie Coleman saved for passage to France and earned her international pilot's license there in just seven months, becoming the first African-American woman — and first Native American — to hold one. She flew airshows across the U.S. billed as "Queen Bess," refusing to perform anywhere that segregated its audience, before her career was cut short by a fatal accident in 1926.

Sep 3, 1989Varig Flight 254Amazon Rainforest, Brazil

Downed By A Decimal Point

— September 1989

A single mistyped heading — 270° instead of the intended 027.0° — sent Varig Flight 254 deep into the Amazon rainforest, hundreds of miles off course, until it ran out of fuel. Captain Cézar Garcez chose to belly-land in the jungle canopy rather than risk an explosion on a hard landing; 12 of the 54 aboard died, but the rest were rescued from the wreckage two days later.

NTSB Verified
Oct 25, 1999Learjet 35, N47BASouth Dakota, USA

The Ghost Flight

— October 1999

Golfer Payne Stewart's chartered Learjet climbed toward Dallas and then, at around 23,000 feet, stopped responding to air traffic control entirely. Fighter jets scrambled to intercept found the aircraft's windows frosted opaque from a loss of cabin pressure and everyone aboard apparently unconscious. The jet flew on, unresponsive, for nearly four hours before running out of fuel and crashing in South Dakota, a case that remains a grim benchmark for how quickly hypoxia disables a flight deck.

Verified
Aug 14, 2005Helios Airways Flight 522Grammatiko, Greece

The Cockpit That Went Silent

— August 2005

A pressurization switch left in "manual" after ground maintenance meant Helios Flight 522's cabin never pressurized after takeoff. Both pilots were incapacitated by hypoxia before they identified the fault, and the aircraft flew on autopilot for over an hour, tracked helplessly by intercepting fighter jets, until fuel ran out. The case remains one of the clearest arguments in aviation for redundant, unmistakable warning systems.

Captain Robert Pearson gets most of the credit for the Gimli Glider, but it was First Officer Maurice Quintal who suggested the decommissioned airstrip at Gimli in the first place — he'd been stationed there years earlier as an Air Force pilot and simply remembered it existed. Dec 29, 1972Eastern Air Lines Flight 401Everglades, Florida

The Burnt-Out Bulb That Brought Down A Jet

— December 1972

A single burnt-out landing-gear indicator light became such a distraction that Eastern Air Lines Flight 401's entire flight crew missed the autopilot silently disconnecting beneath them. The L-1011 descended, undetected, into the Everglades at cruise speed, killing 101 of the 176 aboard. The accident became one of the founding case studies behind Crew Resource Management — the same discipline that, seventeen years later, helped save United 232.
