Evolution

The Evolution of Flight

From Kitty Hawk to classified — the aircraft that changed everything. Sixteen machines, twelve seconds of first flight and over a century later, hypersonic prototypes that may already be airborne. This is the story of how we learned to fly, how fast we learned to fly in secret, and why so many of the “UFOs” people reported over the decades had a hangar and a serial number.

December 17, 1903 — Kitty Hawk, North Carolina
1903

Wright Flyer

The first powered flight in human history
First Flight

Orville and Wilbur Wright were bicycle mechanics with no formal engineering training and no pilot's license to their name — because no such thing existed yet. What they had was five years of glider experiments, a homemade wind tunnel, and a stubborn refusal to accept that heavier-than-air flight was impossible.

  • 12 seconds airborne, 120 feet traveled — shorter than the wingspan of a modern Boeing 747
  • Four flights that morning; the longest, piloted by Wilbur, covered 852 feet in 59 seconds
  • Only five people witnessed it, one of whom took the single photograph that now defines the moment
  • Every aircraft on this page, from airliners to stealth bombers, descends directly from this twelve-second flight

May 20–21, 1927 — New York to Paris
1927

Spirit of St. Louis

Lindbergh's solo, nonstop Atlantic crossing
Solo Milestone

Charles Lindbergh took off from Roosevelt Field with sandwiches, canteens of water, and no radio, no parachute and no forward visibility — the fuel tank was mounted directly ahead of the cockpit, so he flew much of the crossing looking out a periscope or a side window.

  • 33.5 hours in the air, alone, fighting sleep deprivation and icing over open ocean
  • Won the $25,000 Orteig Prize, offered since 1919 for the first nonstop New York–Paris flight
  • Landed at Le Bourget Field to a crowd of over 150,000 people
  • Turned aviation overnight from a dangerous curiosity into a global obsession, kicking off a commercial aviation boom

October 14, 1947 — Mojave Desert, California
1947

Bell X-1

Chuck Yeager breaks the sound barrier
Speed Record

Chuck Yeager flew with two broken ribs, sustained two days earlier in a horseback riding accident he hid from his superiors for fear of being pulled from the flight. He used a sawed-off broom handle to lever the cockpit hatch shut because he couldn't reach it with his injured arm.

  • Reached Mach 1.06 at 45,000 feet — the first confirmed supersonic flight by a human pilot
  • The bright orange rocket plane was air-launched from the belly of a modified B-29 bomber
  • Named “Glamorous Glennis” after Yeager's wife, painted on the aircraft's nose
  • Proved supersonic flight was survivable and controllable, ending fears of an impassable “sound barrier”

1952 — London, United Kingdom
1952

De Havilland Comet

The world's first commercial jet airliner
Jet Age

BOAC's Comet cut flight times dramatically and flew smoother and quieter than any propeller airliner before it — passengers described the experience as revolutionary. Then, within two years, three Comets broke apart in mid-air with no warning, grounding the entire fleet.

  • Investigators traced the disasters to metal fatigue around the aircraft's distinctive square windows, where stress concentrated at the sharp corners
  • The failures rewrote aircraft engineering standards worldwide — every jet airliner since has had rounded windows
  • Despite the tragedies, the Comet proved jet travel was viable and paved the way for the 707 and every jetliner after it
  • A hard, costly lesson that pressurized fuselages fail in ways propeller aircraft never had to consider

1955 — Lockheed Skunk Works, Burbank, California
1955

Lockheed U-2

Built to fly above Soviet missiles — and still flying today
Classified Program Still Flying Today
Cold War Spy Plane

Designed and built in secret by Lockheed's Skunk Works division in just 88 days, the U-2 was built to do one thing: fly higher than anything the Soviet Union could shoot down. At 70,000 feet, pilots wear full pressure suits nearly identical to astronaut gear, because the air is too thin to sustain life without one.

  • Cruises above 70,000 feet — high enough that early Soviet missiles and interceptors couldn't reach it
  • Widely regarded as the hardest aircraft in the world to land, with a stall speed so close to its landing speed that a chase car with a second pilot radios altitude calls down to the pilot on every approach
  • Francis Gary Powers' U-2 was shot down over the USSR in 1960, triggering a major Cold War diplomatic crisis and exposing the covert overflight program
  • Seventy years after its first flight, the U-2 is still flying operational reconnaissance missions today

1962 — Area 51, Nevada
1962

A-12 Oxcart

The CIA's fastest spy plane — piloted by its own officers
CIA Black Project Declassified 2007
CIA Black Project

The A-12 wasn't flown by the Air Force — its pilots were CIA officers, recruited quietly and sworn to a level of secrecy so total that many couldn't tell their own families what they did for a living. Developed and tested out of Area 51, the program's very existence generated hundreds of UFO reports from people who had no idea what they were looking at.

  • Cruised at Mach 3.35 — actually faster than the SR-71 that succeeded it
  • Only 12 were built, and three pilots were killed in crashes during the program's short life
  • Titanium airframe construction meant the aircraft ran hot enough at cruise speed to expand several inches in flight
  • The CIA didn't officially declassify the A-12 program until 2007 — 45 years after its first flight

December 22, 1964 — Palmdale, California
1964

SR-71 Blackbird

The fastest air-breathing aircraft ever built
Retired 1998
Speed Record

Built from an evolved A-12 design, the SR-71 flew so fast that its standard defense against a surface-to-air missile launch was simply to accelerate away from it. It could cross the Atlantic in under two hours — a trip that takes a modern airliner nearly seven.

  • Cruised at Mach 3.2, roughly 2,200 mph, at altitudes over 85,000 feet
  • Its titanium airframe was largely built from titanium the U.S. purchased from the Soviet Union through shell companies, since the Soviets were the world's largest supplier and never suspected what it was for
  • Retired in 1998 and never officially replaced — no acknowledged aircraft has matched its performance since
  • Still holds the world record for fastest recorded flight by an air-breathing, crewed aircraft

1969 — Toulouse, France & London, United Kingdom
1969

Concorde

The first — and only — supersonic passenger airliner
Supersonic Airliner

A joint British and French program, Concorde first flew in 1969 and entered commercial service in 1976, cutting the London–New York flight time to roughly three and a half hours at twice the speed of sound. Passengers on the westbound crossing would arrive, by the clock, before they'd taken off.

  • Only 20 were ever built, and just a fraction flew commercially for British Airways and Air France
  • The July 2000 crash of Air France Flight 4590 near Paris, which killed all 109 people aboard and four on the ground, badly damaged public confidence in the aircraft
  • Rising maintenance costs, the 2000 crash and the post-9/11 downturn in premium travel led to Concorde's retirement in 2003
  • Over two decades later, no airliner has matched its speed — supersonic passenger travel remains, for now, a closed chapter

February 9, 1969 — Everett, Washington
1969

Boeing 747

The Jumbo Jet that democratized air travel
Still Flying Today
Jumbo Jet

Boeing built the 747 as a hedge — its own engineers weren't sure supersonic airliners like Concorde wouldn't make wide-body subsonic jets obsolete within a decade. Instead, the “Jumbo Jet” became the aircraft that made international travel affordable for millions of ordinary passengers.

  • Its double-deck hump design could carry roughly 500 passengers in a high-density configuration, more than double most airliners at the time
  • The distinctive hump exists because Boeing originally planned to convert the aircraft to a cargo freighter, requiring a nose-loading door and a raised cockpit out of the way
  • Fundamentally changed the economics of commercial aviation, driving down the cost per seat on long-haul international routes
  • Fifty-seven years after its first flight, 747s are still flying passenger and cargo routes worldwide

1977 — Palmdale, California
1977

B-1 Lancer

The variable-sweep-wing strategic bomber
Strategic Bomber

Crews nicknamed the B-1 “The Bone” — a play on “B-One” — and it's earned the name through decades of continuous upgrades since entering service. Its swing-wing design lets it sweep its wings forward for takeoff and landing, then back for high-speed, low-altitude penetration.

  • Capable of Mach 1.25 at low altitude, using terrain-following flight to slip beneath enemy radar coverage
  • Carries the largest conventional payload of any aircraft in the current U.S. Air Force bomber fleet
  • Its variable-geometry wings were a direct response to the design tradeoff between efficient high-altitude cruise and fast, stable low-altitude flight
  • Remains in active service today, decades after its combat debut

1981 — Groom Lake, Nevada
1981

F-117 Nighthawk

The first operational stealth aircraft ever built
Classified Missions Continue
Stealth Pioneer

The Nighthawk's bizarre, angular shape — all flat facets instead of smooth curves — looks strange because it was designed by computer to scatter radar returns, using math from a 1960s paper by Soviet physicist Pyotr Ufimtsev that the U.S. found sitting, untranslated and unrestricted, in a technical library. Lockheed's engineers realized the Soviets themselves hadn't grasped its military potential.

  • Flew combat missions in secret for seven years before its existence was publicly acknowledged in 1988
  • Designated a “fighter” (the “F” in F-117) specifically to attract better pilots to volunteer for it, despite being purely a stealth attack bomber
  • Flew in the 1989 invasion of Panama and the 1991 Gulf War, where its radar-evading strikes changed how air campaigns were planned
  • One F-117 was shot down over Serbia in 1999 after a well-prepared crew tracked it on longer radar wavelengths — recovered wreckage reportedly helped other nations study stealth countermeasures
  • Officially retired in 2008, but F-117s have been photographed flying again in the years since on what the Air Force describes only as continued testing and training

1986 — St. Louis, Missouri
1986

F/A-18 Hornet

The Navy's first true multirole fighter
Still Flying Today
Multirole Fighter

The Hornet was designed to do the job of two separate aircraft, the F-4 Phantom and A-7 Corsair, letting the Navy simplify training and maintenance around a single airframe that could dogfight and drop bombs on the same mission. It's since become one of the most recognizable jets in naval aviation.

  • First aircraft flown by a woman in combat, in 1994, following the repeal of U.S. combat exclusion policies for female pilots
  • Still flies today in its upgraded Super Hornet form, decades after its combat debut
  • In November 2004, F/A-18 pilots training off the coast of San Diego with the USS Nimitz carrier group filmed and reported the now-famous “Tic Tac” encounter — footage the Pentagon later confirmed as authentic
  • That single encounter, more than any other, brought military UAP reports into mainstream public and congressional attention

July 17, 1989 — Palmdale, California
1989

B-2 Spirit

The flying-wing stealth bomber
Stealth Bomber

There's no fuselage, no tail and no vertical stabilizers on a B-2 — the entire aircraft is one seamless flying wing, shaped and coated to be nearly invisible to radar. It remains, dollar for dollar, the most expensive aircraft ever built.

  • Each aircraft cost roughly $2.1 billion, making it the most expensive military aircraft in history
  • Only 21 were ever built, and fewer than 20 remain in the active fleet today
  • Capable of carrying nuclear weapons alongside conventional precision munitions, giving it a global strike role
  • Flown by a crew of just two on missions that can last 30-plus hours with multiple aerial refuelings, striking targets anywhere on Earth from a single home base

1997 — United States Air Force / Space Force
1997

X-37B

The unmanned space plane nobody will explain
Mission Classified
Space Plane

The X-37B looks like a shrunken Space Shuttle and lands like an airplane, but what it actually does once it reaches orbit is one of the Space Force's most closely guarded secrets. Officials confirm launches and landings; they do not confirm payloads or mission objectives.

  • Now operated by the U.S. Space Force after originating as a NASA and Boeing research program
  • Has logged over 900 consecutive days in orbit on a single mission before deorbiting and landing on a runway like a conventional aircraft
  • Unmanned and reusable, it can change its own orbit — something adversary satellite trackers have publicly said makes it difficult to predict or monitor
  • Its classified, unexplained orbital maneuvers are exactly the kind of activity that has fueled modern satellite-related UAP speculation

2013 — Kandahar, Afghanistan
2013

RQ-170 Sentinel

“The Beast of Kandahar”
Captured Intact 2011
Stealth Drone

Long before the public knew its name, ground crews and enthusiast photographers nicknamed the unidentified flying-wing drone spotted over Afghanistan “The Beast of Kandahar.” It shares its tailless, flying-wing silhouette with the much larger B-2 — a design language that shows up again and again across Lockheed and Northrop's stealth programs.

  • Designed to replace the U-2 for some of the highest-risk stealth reconnaissance missions
  • Iran captured one intact in December 2011 after it reportedly went down over Iranian territory, giving foreign engineers rare hands-on access to U.S. stealth drone technology
  • Its flying-wing shape minimizes radar cross-section using the same underlying stealth principles as the B-2 Spirit
  • The U.S. has never fully confirmed the circumstances of the loss, keeping much of the incident officially unresolved

2026 — Skunk Works, Palmdale, California
2026

SR-72 “Darkstar”

The rumored hypersonic successor to the Blackbird
Unconfirmed
Hypersonic (Rumored)

Lockheed Martin has publicly confirmed it is developing an SR-71 successor capable of hypersonic speeds, without confirming a flying prototype exists. “Darkstar,” the sleek hypersonic aircraft featured in Top Gun: Maverick, was built as a movie prop — but Lockheed's own advanced programs executives have said the film's design deliberately echoed real internal SR-72 concept work, blurring the line between marketing and genuine leaked concept art.

  • Targeted cruise speeds above Mach 6 — double the SR-71's top speed
  • No agency has confirmed a crewed or uncrewed test flight has taken place
  • Persistent rumors and unconfirmed sightings suggest classified test flights may already be underway at remote desert test ranges
  • If real, it would represent the direct spiritual successor to the A-12 and SR-71 — continuing seventy years of Skunk Works aircraft built faster and more secretly than the public is told

UAP Connection

Why Spy Planes Caused Thousands of UFO Reports

For decades, altitude was the best-kept secret in aviation. Nobody expected to see an aircraft at 70,000 feet, let alone one shaped like a black dart with no visible engine glow at dusk — so when the U-2, A-12 Oxcart and SR-71 Blackbird began flying test and operational routes over the American Southwest and beyond, air traffic controllers, airline pilots and ordinary civilians reported exactly what they saw: something fast, silent and completely unidentifiable.

The CIA has since confirmed, in its own declassified internal history of the U-2 and Oxcart programs, that these covert test flights accounted for more than half of all UFO reports filed with the Air Force's Project Blue Book between the mid-1950s and 1960s. The government wasn't hiding aliens — it was hiding aircraft, and the secrecy needed to protect those programs meant investigators often couldn't tell witnesses the truth even when they knew it.

Declassified Lockheed Martin — Advanced Development Programs

The Skunk Works Files

Since 1943, one small, deliberately isolated Lockheed engineering division has been responsible for a wildly disproportionate share of the aircraft on this page. Officially named Advanced Development Programs, everyone — including its own employees — just calls it Skunk Works.

Program Summary — ██████████████████

Formed under legendary engineer Kelly Johnson to build America's first jet fighter in 143 days, the division operated under strict rules: small teams, minimal paperwork, and total secrecy from the rest of the company. That same model later produced the U-2, the A-12 Oxcart, the SR-71 Blackbird and the F-117 Nighthawk — each one built faster, higher or stealthier than the public was told was possible at the time.

Hover to reveal — most of this was only declassified decades after the aircraft first flew.

Built in 88 days to fly above Soviet air defenses entirely. Still flying operational missions seventy years later.
