Dec 24, 1971LANSA Flight 508Peruvian Amazon

The Girl Who Fell From The Sky

— December 1971

Seventeen-year-old Juliane Koepcke was still strapped into her seat when lightning tore her LANSA Electra apart in a thunderstorm over the Peruvian Amazon, and she fell nearly two miles to the jungle floor still buckled into the row. Badly injured but alive, and drawing on wilderness knowledge from growing up at her parents' rainforest research station, she survived eleven days alone in the jungle before finding a logging camp.

The Twelve-Year-Old Who Survived Nine Hours At Sea

— June 2009

Bahia Bakari was twelve, travelling with her mother, when their A310 crashed into the Indian Ocean approaching Comoros, killing all 152 other people aboard. With no life vest and little swimming experience, she clung to a piece of wreckage for more than nine hours, much of it in total darkness, before a passing boat spotted her. The world press called her simply "the miracle girl."

Aug 16, 1987Northwest Airlines Flight 255Detroit, Michigan

The Four-Year-Old Who Was The Only One Who Lived

— August 1987

Cecelia Cichan was four years old, strapped into her seat next to her parents and older brother, when Northwest Flight 255 crashed on takeoff from Detroit, killing 156 people — her entire family among them. Firefighters found her still in her seat, badly injured but alive, the sole survivor of the crash. She spoke publicly about the crash for the first time in 2011, after choosing for decades to live privately with what the press had called her.

Jan 21, 1985Galaxy Airlines Flight 203Reno, Nevada

The Only Survivor Of Seventy-One

— January 1985

George Lamson Jr. was seventeen, flying with his father to a Super Bowl weekend, when Galaxy Airlines Flight 203 crashed shortly after takeoff from Reno, breaking apart just in front of their row. His father survived the initial impact but died of injuries soon after, along with everyone else aboard — leaving Lamson the sole survivor of 71 people. He has since spent much of his life supporting other "sole survivors" through the same specific, isolating grief.

Jan 15, 2009US Airways Flight 1549Hudson River, NY

What It Was Like In The Cabin On The Hudson

— January 2009

Passengers aboard US Airways 1549 describe the same sequence: a loud bang, the smell of fuel, total engine silence, and Captain Sullenberger's four words over the PA — "brace for impact." Then a hard, controlled splash into the Hudson River, and a calm, fast evacuation onto the wings as ferries arrived within minutes. All 155 people aboard survived, in what remains one of the most complete rescue operations in aviation history.

The Hijacker Who Jumped Into A Legend

— November 1971

A man calling himself Dan Cooper hijacked a 727, collected $200,000 in ransom and four parachutes, then jumped from the rear stairs somewhere over the Pacific Northwest — and was never identified or found. How Common In-Flight Medical Emergencies Actually Are

— NEJM, 2013

A landmark New England Journal of Medicine study of nearly 12,000 in-flight medical calls across five airlines found a medical emergency occurs roughly once every 604 flights — most commonly fainting, breathing trouble or nausea — and that a physician passenger happened to step in to help about half the time. Only about 7% of those emergencies led to a diversion.

Jan 13, 1982Air Florida Flight 90Potomac River, Washington D.C.

The Sixth Passenger

— January 1982

After Air Florida Flight 90 crashed into the icy Potomac River, six people survived the initial impact, clinging to wreckage in freezing water. Each time a rescue helicopter lowered its line, passenger Arland Williams passed it to someone else instead of himself — five times. By the time the helicopter returned a sixth time, he had slipped under the water. He was the only one of the 78 who died that day whose cause of death was drowning, not impact. The bridge at the crash site was later renamed in his honor.
