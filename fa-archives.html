Sep 8, 2015British Airways Flight 2276Las Vegas, NV

The Evacuation That Took Ninety Seconds

— September 2015

When British Airways Flight 2276's engine suffered an uncontained failure and caught fire during its takeoff roll in Las Vegas, cabin crew evacuated all 170 people down the slides before fire crews even had the blaze fully under control. Investigators later pointed to the crew's calm, drilled response as the reason there wasn't a single serious injury.

Sep 2, 1998Swissair Flight 111Off Nova Scotia, Canada

The Crew That Smelled Smoke Before Any Alarm Sounded

— September 1998

An hour out of New York, the cabin crew of Swissair Flight 111 noticed an unusual smell in the cockpit before any warning system flagged it, prompting the crew to calmly radio a "Pan Pan Pan" and divert toward Halifax. The fire that followed spread faster than anyone anticipated, but the crew's early instinct — trusting their own senses over the instruments — remains a textbook example taught in cabin safety training.

Aug 2, 2005Air France Flight 358Toronto, Canada

All 309 Walked Off A Burning Airbus

— August 2005

Landing in a severe thunderstorm, an Air France A340 overran the runway at Toronto Pearson and came to rest in a ravine, catching fire on impact. With one evacuation slide punctured by debris and the weather still raging, cabin crew got all 297 passengers and 12 crew off the burning aircraft in about ninety seconds — a survival rate investigators called close to miraculous given the severity of the crash.

Aug 14, 2005Helios Airways Flight 522Grammatiko, Greece

The Flight Attendant Who Held A Commercial Pilot's License

— August 2005

After both pilots of Helios Flight 522 were incapacitated by a pressurization failure, flight attendant Andreas Prodromou — who held a UK commercial pilot's license but wasn't qualified on the Boeing 737 — made his way to the flight deck and attempted to regain control with the little time and oxygen he had left. He didn't have the type-specific training to save the flight, but his attempt is remembered as one of the most extraordinary things any cabin crew member has ever tried mid-emergency.

Jan 26, 1972JAT Flight 367Srbská Kamenice, Czechoslovakia

She Fell 33,000 Feet — And Lived

— January 1972

After an in-flight explosion tore JAT Flight 367 apart over Czechoslovakia, flight attendant Vesna Vulović fell nearly six miles trapped inside a section of the fuselage. She was found still alive in the wreckage, and after a long recovery, she was recognized by Guinness World Records for surviving the highest fall without a parachute ever recorded.

Since 2004All U.S. Commercial AircraftFederal Requirement

The Defibrillator Every Crew Is Trained To Use

— Federal requirement since 2004

Since 2004, the FAA has required every U.S. commercial aircraft with a flight attendant aboard to carry an automated external defibrillator, and every flight attendant is certified in CPR and AED use as part of basic training. It's rarely mentioned in the safety demo, but it's one of the reasons in-flight cardiac emergencies aren't automatically fatal anymore.

Federal aviation regulations set a minimum number of flight attendants an aircraft must carry based on passenger seating capacity, full stop — it's a safety requirement, not a service amenity, which is why crew stay standing and briefing until the exact required staffing and safety checks are satisfied on every single flight.

The Regulation Behind Every Layover

— Federal regulation

Federal rules require flight attendants scheduled for a duty period of up to 14 hours to get at least 10 consecutive hours of rest before their next shift, rising to 12 hours for longer duty periods — one of the least visible reasons a layover exists at all, and a hard floor no airline can schedule around.
